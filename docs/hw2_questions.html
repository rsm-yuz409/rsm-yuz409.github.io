Replication of Card and Krueger (1994)

Minimum Wages and Employment:A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industryin New Jersey and Pennsylvania

Author

Yunqing Zhao

Published

April 22, 2026

Introduction

Card and Krueger (1994) study whether raising the minimum wage reduces employment in fast food restaurants. Their paper focuses on a policy change in New Jersey, where the state minimum wage increased from $4.25 to $5.05 in April 1992.

To study the effect of this policy, the authors compare fast food restaurants in New Jersey to similar restaurants in eastern Pennsylvania, where the minimum wage did not change. This gives them a treatment group and a control group.

This question matters because the standard textbook prediction is that a higher minimum wage should reduce employment. But Card and Krueger find something very different: employment does not fall, and may even rise slightly in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania.

Main Analysis

In this section, I replicate the main difference-in-differences result from Table 3 in Card and Krueger (1994). This is the key result of the paper since it compares how employment changed in New Jersey after the minimum wage increase relative to employment in eastern Pennsylvania, where the minimum wage did not change.

Following the paper, I measure employment using full-time equivalent employment (FTE), which is defined as the number of full-time workers plus one half of the number of part-time workers. I then compare average FTE employment before and after the policy change in the two states.

The goal is to reproduce the basic DID estimate reported in the paper and check whether the data lead to a result close to the published finding.

The replicated values are very close to those reported in Table 2 of the paper. In wave 1, employment levels are lower in New Jersey than in Pennsylvania, while starting wages are very similar across the two states. The replicated results closely match those reported in Table 3 of the paper. In Pennsylvania, employment declined from about 23.33 to 21.17, a change of about -2.17. In contrast, employment in New Jersey increased slightly from about 20.44 to 21.03, a change of about 0.58. The difference-in-differences estimate is about 2.75, which is nearly identical to the published value of 2.76. This indicates that employment in New Jersey increased relative to Pennsylvania after the minimum wage increase.

Something Additional

The importance of DID analysis

The difference-in-differences (DID) approach is important in amalysis since it focuses on changes over time rather than just comparing levels. In this paper, New Jersey is the treatment group and Pennsylvania is the control group.

The main idea is that DID helps remove factors that affect both states in the same way over time. For example, broader economic conditions, like a recession, would influence employment in both places. By comparing how employment changes in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania, these shared time effects are removed.

DID also controls for fixed differences between the two states. Even if New Jersey and Pennsylvania started at different employment levels, those differences are not a problem because we are looking at changes instead of levels.

Overall, the DID design helps isolate the effect of the policy from other factors, making the results more convincing as a causal estimate.
